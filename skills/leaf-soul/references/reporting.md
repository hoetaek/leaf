# Reporting — how to brief a decision-maker

The depth behind leaf-soul's Reporting rule, with the references it draws on. The
SKILL.md Reporting section is the short standard the LEAF skills follow; this file
is the gathered material and its sources.

## The one rule everything converges on: answer first

BLUF, the Minto Pyramid, SCQA, and executive-summary practice independently land
on the same move — **lead with the conclusion, put support underneath.** A
decision-maker reads top-down and outcome-first and may never read past the
summary, so the summary is where they decide whether to act.

## The frameworks (models to emulate)

- **BLUF — Bottom Line Up Front** (military / public-safety standard). Put the most
  important point in the first sentence: "if the reader saw only this line, what
  must they know?" Active voice; every word earns its place. It is not an abstract
  — it is the conclusion itself, stated first, then the context that led to it.
- **Minto Pyramid Principle** (Barbara Minto, McKinsey). One assertion at the top
  → ~3 supporting arguments → evidence/data at the base. Each layer answers the
  "so what?" of the layer below, so the reader can assess, challenge, and act
  quickly.
- **SCQA — Situation · Complication · Question · Answer** (Minto's opening
  structure). A little context, the change that made this report necessary, the
  question it raises, then the answer. Orients the reader without preamble.
- **Executive-summary practice.** Order is conclusion → context → evidence.
  Findings in descending significance, 3–5 of them, each 1–2 sentences.
  Recommendations in priority / impact order — highest or fastest value first.
  Roughly one page of summary per ten pages of detail; board-level ~one page.

## The shape of a good brief

1. **Bottom line** — the conclusion and what you need from the reader, in one or
   two plain-language sentences.
2. **Why it matters** — a little context and the complication that triggered it.
3. **Findings / Verify · Decide** — the few things that need the reader's eyes, in
   descending significance, each *self-contained*: the point, its impact, and the
   action.
4. **Recommendations** — in priority order; each states the action *and* the
   expected result ("do X, so that Y").
5. **Evidence / detail** — beneath, organized and viewable for drilling into;
   never dumped on top.

## Anti-patterns (cautionary — what makes a report fail)

- **Labels instead of statements** — "MEDIUM 2: frontmatter" tells the reader
  nothing. State the problem, its impact, and the action in plain words.
- **Author's-head vocabulary** — words that land only because you did the work:
  your own category names ("inside/outside"), file paths, framework shorthand. The
  reader should not have to decode your head; translate to plain terms or cut.
- **Buried conclusion** — making the reader hunt for the "so what".
- **Unsorted dump** — raw material with no descending-significance order.
- **Recommendation without consequence** — "do X" with no "so that Y".
- **Passive, hedged, jargon-heavy prose.**
